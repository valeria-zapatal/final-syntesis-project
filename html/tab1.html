Why is it so complicated to detect an exoplanet?

Detecting an exoplanet is tricky because planets are much smaller and faint even faster than the stars they orbit, making them hard to see directly. Also, planets do not emit their own light, but instead reflect light from their star, making them even more difficult to detect. The most common techniques and their limitations, Transit method, Radial velocity method, Gravitational microlensing method, Direct images.

What is a planet itself?

A planet is a body that orbits around a star and that has enough mass to have a spherical shape due to gravity. Originally, the planets were considered objects that moved with respect to the stars and did not appear to have a regular shape. A planet meets three criteria: it must orbit a star, it must have enough mass to be spherical in shape due to gravity, it must have cleared its orbit of other objects of similar size. If an object meets these three criteria, it is considered a planet. If it meets only the first two criteria, but not the third, it is classified as a “dwarf planet.”



The closest exoplanet and considered the strongest candidate for habitability is Proxima Centauri b, which is located at a distance of approximately 4.2 light-years from Earth in the habitable zone of its star. However, despite being the closest exoplanet and having the possibility of being habitable, very little is still known about its size, composition, and physical characteristics. The gravitational acceleration of Proxima Centauri b will depend on its mass and size, which are not yet known with certainty. Its radius is also unknown, but it is estimated to have a mass similar to that of Earth. Regarding its composition, Proxima Centauri b is believed to be a rocky planet, similar in composition to Earth.
